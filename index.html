About the data — what we use

Everything here is built from two free, public, no-key datasets plus one figure we compute ourselves. Nothing is paid, scraped, or behind a login.

What we deliberately don't use: shots, possession, xG, cards, or the official FIFA API. Those don't exist for free across all the matches we need (friendlies and minor-confederation qualifiers), so rather than show a half-empty table we lean on the single highest-signal free signal — results, distilled into Elo. Goal/scorer figures follow the openfootball dataset and can differ slightly from official records where contributor data is incomplete (pre-2014 goal detail is sparse).

How the forecast is built — the math

1 · Elo rating. Every nation carries a strength rating, updated after each match by R′ = R + K · G · (W − Wₑ), where the expected result is Wₑ = 1 / (1 + 10^(−d/400)) and d is the rating gap plus a +100 home-field bonus (0 at a neutral venue). W is the actual result (1 win / ½ draw / 0 loss). K weights a match by importance — World Cup 60, continental finals 50, World-Cup/continental qualifiers & Nations League 40, minor tournaments 30, friendlies 20 — and G is a margin-of-victory multiplier (1 for a 1-goal win, 1.5 for 2, (11+gd)/8 for 3+). We run this over every men's international since 1872 (~49,000 matches) to get today's ratings.

2 · Live conditioning. Ratings are frozen the day before the tournament, then nudged by the actual 2026 results as they come in. In the simulation, played group results are locked and only the unplayed fixtures are simulated; once knockout games are played, their winners are locked too.

3 · The goal model. For each simulated group match, each team's expected goals scale with the rating gap d (including any host bump): λ = base · 10^(±γ·d/400), with goals drawn from an independent Poisson. base and γ aren't guessed — they're maximum-likelihood-fit to the recent competitive scorelines we already hold (currently ≈ 1.15 and 0.33). Because the exponent works both ways, a mismatch produces more total goals — and the occasional 4-0 — instead of a fixed-total game. Knockout winners come straight from the Elo win-probability (penalties make scorelines a poor guide in one-off games).

4 · The bracket. Top 2 of each group plus the 8 best third-placed teams advance (32 of 48), slotted into the official Round-of-32 → Final bracket; hosts (USA/Canada/Mexico) get a +50 Elo home bump in their matches. We then run this whole thing 50,000 times (30,000 for the in-browser refresh).

5 · Betting-market blend (when enabled). We pull two de-vigged the-odds-api markets and feed both into the ratings: the outright-winner market is regressed into Elo units and each team is pulled halfway toward it; the match (h2h) odds for upcoming games give an extra pairwise nudge. So the market's read on injuries, form and squad changes flows through every number — title odds, group-stage exit (which is just 1 − reach-knockout), road to the final, all of it. We publish our resulting probabilities, not the raw odds. The Title Odds panel marks the market's number (▏) next to ours.

What is a “Monte-Carlo forecast”?

Rather than guess a single outcome, we play out the rest of the tournament 50,000 times. Each match isn't decided by who's “better” — it's a dice roll, but a loaded one: the stronger team (by Elo, now market-informed) is more likely to win, by an amount the maths above sets. Run tens of thousands of these full tournaments and you get tens of thousands of different champions, finalists and group tables.

The percentages you see are simply how often each thing happened: if a team wins the trophy in 11,500 of 50,000 simulated tournaments, that's a 23% title chance. It's named after the Monte-Carlo casino — it uses randomness on purpose. More simulations make the numbers steadier (the wobble shrinks like 1/√N), which is exactly what the confidence score below measures.
